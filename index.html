The jade lake at the end of the pavement.

Ten-odd minutes past Mount Currie the asphalt gives up, the Lillooet River fans into its delta, and a huge milky-green lake fills the valley — rimmed by a gravel forest road, a string of rustic camps, and almost nothing else. This is a logbook for driving it slowly and paddling it early.

Entry I · The Road In

Washboard is a speed, not a problem.

Pemberton ends the way all good valleys do — gradually, then suddenly. You pass through Mount Currie, and about ten minutes on, the road drops toward the head of the lake, where the Lillooet River unbraids itself into a wide silty delta and pours everything the glaciers gave it into twenty-some kilometres of green water. The colour is the first thing everyone mentions and the last thing anyone gets tired of: a milky, opaque jade that doesn’t look real in photographs and looks even less real in person. It’s rock flour — glacial silt ground fine, hanging in the water column, bending the light.

The east shore belongs to the In-SHUCK-ch Forest Service Road: gravel, dusty, corrugated into washboard for long stretches, and entirely fair about it. Driven slowly, it’s fine for most vehicles in decent conditions. Driven like pavement, it will rattle your dashboard into a maraca and your passengers into a mutiny. Twenty to forty clicks, windows up on the dusty bits, headlights on, and pull right for anything big — this is an active industrial road and logging traffic has the weight advantage and the right of way.

“The washboard is the toll. You pay it in patience, and the lake pays you back in a colour you can’t buy anywhere on pavement.” Logbook, Vol. II

Know what you’re driving into: there are no services down the lake. No fuel, no store, no taps, and little or no cell coverage once you’re committed. Bring water, bring a spare you’ve actually checked, and tell someone where you’re going. And remember what the road is: it continues past the lake toward the communities of the In-SHUCK-ch Nations, and the whole valley sits in Líl̓wat territory. You are driving through a working road and a homeland, not a playground — behave like the guest you are.

Road notes, condensed

  • Surface — washboard gravel; fine driven slowly, punishing driven fast.
  • Traffic — logging & industrial trucks; lights on, pull right, give way.
  • Dust — real. Windows up when anything passes.
  • Services — none past the pavement. Fuel up in Pemberton.
  • Cell — assume none. Leave a trip plan with someone.
  • Conditions — change with weather and road work; check current status before you go.
Entry II · Logbook of the Rec Sites

Three camps, one gravel road, zero frills.

Strung along the east shore are rustic BC forest-recreation campsites — the old-school kind: a clearing, a lake, maybe a pit toilet, and whatever you brought. Historically first-come, first-served. Fees and current status shift over the years, so verify with Recreation Sites & Trails BC before you build a weekend on it.

01

Strawberry Point

East shore · In-SHUCK-ch FSR

The name overpromises fruit and the site delivers something better: lakeshore camping close enough to the water that the jade is the first thing you see when the tent unzips. A favourite for the drive-in-and-flop crowd — which also means summer weekends fill early. Arrive mid-week or arrive with a plan B.

Twin One Creek

East shore · In-SHUCK-ch FSR

Farther down the washboard, where a creek comes off the eastern slopes to meet the lake. The extra distance filters the crowd — the people who make it this far tend to be the ones who wanted quiet in the first place. Same rustic deal: pack in everything, pack out more than you brought.

Lizzie Bay

East shore · In-SHUCK-ch FSR

Down toward the far reaches of the lake, Lizzie Bay is the classic reward-for-persistence camp — a sheltered curve of shoreline at the end of a long rattle. It’s also the jumping-off name for the country behind it, which climbs toward alpine that’s beyond this logbook’s pay grade. Check access and conditions before building bigger plans on it.

Wherever you stop: fire rules change with the season (check current bans), pit toilets are a privilege not a guarantee, and every scrap you carry in comes back out with you.

Entry III · On the Water

Beautiful, and completely unsentimental about it.

Lillooet Lake is a serious piece of water wearing a gorgeous colour. Two facts run the whole show: the water is glacial-cold all year, and the afternoon valley wind builds whitecaps faster than you can say “we should probably head back.”

Rule one

Mornings are the window.

Calm water lives before noon. Launch early, enjoy the glass, and plan to be off — or hugging the shore on your way home — before the valley wind fills in. If whitecaps are already showing, the day’s paddle is over, not “challenging.”

Rule two

Stay near shore.

The lake is big and cold, and the middle of it offers nothing but exposure. Trace the shoreline — it’s where the scenery is anyway — and keep your escape routes short. A capsize in glacial water is a countdown, not an inconvenience.

Rule three

PFDs on. Always.

Not stowed, not “within reach” — worn. Cold-water immersion steals your coordination in minutes, and a lifejacket you’re not wearing is cargo. This is the whole safety sermon; it’s short because it’s non-negotiable.

Beyond the paddling: people fish here, people swim here (briefly — it’s a glacier-fed lake, and every swim ends in a sprint for a warm rock), and in autumn the valley puts on genuinely legendary colour, with peaks of the Joffre group visible up-valley over the delta. Bring a camera and low expectations of leaving on schedule.

This is a bring-your-own-boat lake — there’s no rental shack at the end of a forest road, and that’s part of the charm. If you’re coming up the corridor without a canoe, you can arrange one in Squamish through Squamish Canoe Rental (stable tandem canoes, up to three paddlers per boat, book online) and carry it north with you. Just hold the same discipline here you would anywhere: early starts, shorelines, lifejackets.

Entry IV · When to Go

A year on the jade.

SeasonThe lakeThe fine print
Spring Melt season — the silt plume blooms and the jade turns up to full volume. Water at its coldest. Road can be soft or rough after winter; check conditions. Snow lingers on everything above the valley.
Summer Prime time. Warm camps, glassy mornings, swims measured in seconds. Weekends fill the known sites early. Afternoon wind is at its most reliable — plan around it, not against it.
Fall The legendary one. Cottonwood gold along the delta, Joffre-group peaks dusted white up-valley, almost nobody around mid-week. Shorter days and colder nights; the rustic camps feel very rustic. Worth it.
Winter Quiet, stern, and beautiful when you can get there at all. Access depends entirely on road conditions — do not assume the FSR is drivable. Check before committing.
Entry V · The Honest Note

What the brochures leave out.

There are no brochures, which is the first honest thing about the place. There is also no cell signal, no store, no rescue that arrives quickly, and no version of the road that doesn’t rattle. The dust gets into everything you own. The famous jade water is famous partly because it’s too cold to enjoy for more than a minute at a time. On a summer Saturday, the named camps can be full by lunch.

And the deeper honesty: this valley is Líl̓wat territory, and the road that gives you access is a working route to the communities of the In-SHUCK-ch Nations. People live down this road; industry works down this road. The lake doesn’t owe visitors anything. Come with your patience, your own water, and your garbage bag going home heavier than it came — and it’s one of the finest places in the corridor to do absolutely nothing much at all.

Entry VI · Asked Often

Questions from the pavement.

Can a regular car make it to Lillooet Lake?

Generally yes, driven slowly. The In-SHUCK-ch FSR is washboard gravel — rough and dusty but usually passable for a careful two-wheel-drive car in fair conditions. Slow right down, keep your headlights on, and give way to logging and industrial traffic. Conditions change with weather and road work, so check current status before you commit, especially outside summer.

Do the campsites take reservations?

The camps along the lake — Strawberry Point, Twin One Creek and Lizzie Bay — are rustic BC forest-recreation sites, historically first-come, first-served. Fees, services and reservability have changed over the years, so check the current Recreation Sites & Trails BC status before you go rather than trusting an old forum post (or this page).

Why is the water that jade-green colour?

Glacial silt. The Lillooet River drains glaciated country and carries fine rock flour into the lake at its delta, and those suspended particles scatter light into the milky jade the lake is famous for. It’s most striking in bright light during the melt season.

Is Lillooet Lake safe to paddle?

It can be a beautiful paddle, but treat it as serious water. The lake is glacial-cold, and strong afternoon valley winds build whitecaps fast on a big fetch. Paddle in the morning, stay near the shoreline, wear your PFD at all times, and be off the water before the wind fills in. There are no services and little or no cell coverage if something goes wrong.

Is there cell service down the lake?

Expect little to none once you’re down the In-SHUCK-ch road, and no services of any kind — no fuel, no store, no water taps. Bring everything you need, tell someone your plan, and don’t count on a phone to bail you out.

Can you swim in it?

Briefly. It’s a glacier-fed lake, so even in August the water stays cold enough that most swims are short, loud, and followed by a fast retreat to a warm rock. On a hot Pemberton afternoon, that’s exactly the point.

When’s the best time to visit?

Summer mornings for calm water and warm camps; fall for the colours, which are genuinely legendary in this valley — cottonwood gold along the delta with peaks of the Joffre group visible up-valley. Winter access depends entirely on road conditions; check before trying.

Whose territory is this?

The lake lies in Líl̓wat territory, and the In-SHUCK-ch Forest Service Road continues past the lake toward the communities of the In-SHUCK-ch Nations. It’s a working road and a homeland, not a playground — drive respectfully, camp lightly, and leave every site cleaner than you found it.
